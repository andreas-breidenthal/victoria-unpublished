Victoria Unpublished · Chapter 9

The Bedchamber

Chapter 9

I started with the one fixed line I trusted: The real cutting is elsewhere: the Household made to match the Whigs, the reliance on Melbourne named, the pledge of silence.

I opened the laptop. The screen cast a cold light. I typed: Bedchamber Crisis 1839.

The outline formed quickly. Melbourne resigns after a narrow Commons vote; Peel is sent for; he will take office only if the Queen replaces several Ladies of the Bedchamber — Whig kin — with women aligned to his party. The Queen refuses. Peel declines. Melbourne returns. A constitutional hinge disguised as a quarrel about rooms.

I thought of Ashcombe's twelve days in 1837 — the Household “made to match the Whigs,” the reliance named, the pledge of silence. Two years later, 1839, Peel demands a correction. If those earlier leaves exist — if they say what Ashcombe says they say — the Bedchamber row stops being a naivety tale about a young sovereign's feelings. It becomes a continuity problem: the 1837 formation is acknowledged in private and protected; the 1839 reformation is resisted in public. The symmetry is neat — and lethal.

I wrote it like a mechanic: June–July 1837: formation; Household complexion and instruction under Melbourne. May 1839: Bedchamber confrontation; Peel insists; Victoria refuses; Melbourne returns. 1841: an election settles it; Peel comes in; changes proceed.

I shut the browser. What am I chasing? Not gossip. A design choice at the start of a reign — how a court should look and what that look should do — and the later refusal to let someone else adjust the design. If Ashcombe's fragments are real, they don't just embarrass. They re-wire how the opening chapter is read.

And that is why Mercer is a problem. If he thinks I'm not hunting curios but fault lines, the meeting shifts from offers to containment. He will bring a velvet word and a soft box. Thursday is close.
